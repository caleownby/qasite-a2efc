# Moving Misalignment Into Momentum

Teaser:
An A-Team of engineers builds a product with a tight deadline and high cost for failure. the project gets behind due to changing landscape and a lack of alignment. Will they deliver the product on time, or will they cost the company $300,000 in service fees?


Intro:
The team was hand picked for this project- they had 9 months to build an in-house SSO to replace a deprecating third-party vendor. Missing the deadline would cost the department $50,000 per month.

The product team spent 3 months in requirements gathering, identifying the needs of the department, the use cases of the customers, and the tech stack they needed to integrate with. With a road map and clear, achievable milestones set ahead of them- they began work at a brisk pace.

The challenge:
Within a month, the team was already missing milestones, they were failing to deliver what management was asking for and they were falling further behind by the week. Frustration grew for everyone, communication broke down, trust eroded, and micromanagement increased.

I was brought in to get the team aligned so we could minimize the highway robbery we were facing from our third-party vendor.

The solution:
To address the break down in communication, I implemented a structured product review meeting every other week encompassing the engineers, management, and stakeholders where:
- The conversation focused on **outcomes**, rather than **output** ('what we built' over 'what we did')
- The engineers could showcase those outcomes, proving they were delivering on their commitments.
- Stakeholders could identify and clarify the current, most-valuable outcomes with the engineers- creating achievable short term goals and side-stepping wasteful and non-vital features.
- The clear outcome goals gave the engineers autonomy, eliminating the need for micromanagement and freeing leadership to focus on higher-level work.

The results:
- Management saw real progress on the outcomes that were actually important to them and felt more confident in the team's work.
- The engineers re-engaged with the work, became interested and focused on the product again, increased speed and quality followed.
- The project moved from "6 months behind schedule" to delivering on-time saving the department over $300,000 in third-party vendor fees.

Lessons learned:
- Without constant and clear alignment on the most valuable outcomes, it is easy for engineers and management to end up working in two different directions. Create regular alignment conversations inspecting the actual work-in-progress product and discuss in no uncertain terms what the next steps are to move from where you are today to where you want to be next.
- Micromanagement is an easy tool to reach for when projects or teams get out of hand, but it often slows work down even further. Instead seek alignment with your team around your goals and what success looks like. Support and empower your team to make decisions in line with your vision (course correct when needed) and the team can develop solutions without monopolizing your time making every detailed decision.
- Landscapes are always changing, rarely do we work on the *exact* same product with the *exact* same goals for more than a month before they shift. Expect and plan for the shift, create regular alignment sessions, and deliver frequently so you can pivot when needed.
